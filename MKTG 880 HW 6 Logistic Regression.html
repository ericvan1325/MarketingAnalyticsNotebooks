Section 4: Which Factors are Important/What Factors Drive Choice?¶

  • Given the results from this dataset, it is not certain that any of these factors have a statistically significant impact on Choice, as all of the p-values for each of the predictors are greater than .05, and even still greater than 0.10, so we cant really draw any meaningful statistical conclusions about factors that influence choice.
  • My guess is that there is a significant amount of multicolinearity between the variables, and that might be why we are not having any of the variables be statistically significant. I am going to perform a Likelihood Ratio Test between the null model with just an intercept, and the full model, to see if the coefficients of our predictors are actually zero, or if we have numerical problems like multicolinearity.

In [58]:
LR = -2*(reg2.llf - reg1.llf)
cv = 15.507
print(f'The Chi Squared Critical Value at alpha = 0.05 with {len(reg1.params[1:])} degrees of freedom is {cv}\n')

if LR > cv:
    print(f"{round(LR,3)} is greater than {cv}, thus we reject Ho and conclude that at least one predictor is statistically significantly\nassociated with Customer Choice")
else:
    print(f"Fail to reject Ho. The likelihood ratio statistic is {round(LR,3)}, which is less than the Chi Squared critical value of {cv}.\nWe have no evidence that any of the predictors are statistically significantly associate with customer Choice.")

The Chi Squared Critical Value at alpha = 0.05 with 8 degrees of freedom is 15.507

15.563 is greater than 15.507, thus we reject Ho and conclude that at least one predictor is statistically significantly
associated with Customer Choice
  • So after our Likelihood ratio test, we determined that our overall model was significant, and that at least one predictor was statistically significant, yet we still saw in the full model with all of predictors present that none of them had a p-value smaller than 0.10, and thus were not significant. This seems to me to be more evidence of some numerical problem like multicolinearity, and I also think that makes intuitive sense, because some of the variables are likely capturing the same information, and thus end up cancelling each other out.
